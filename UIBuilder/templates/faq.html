Frequently asked questions

Quick answers about Hurricane Hub. This is still a prototype — always use NWS Tampa Bay and your county for warnings and orders.

What is Hurricane Hub?

A Tampa Bay–focused site that pulls together public weather, water, river, road, and regional data into one snapshot. It helps you orient before you dig into official sources — it is not a forecast and not an evacuation order.

Can I rely on this instead of the government?

No. Use the National Weather Service, your county emergency management, and law enforcement for warnings, evacuations, and closures. Hurricane Hub is a planning aid built from open feeds, which can lag, fail, or disagree.

What does the 0–100 risk score mean?

It’s a single planning index that blends several hazard signals (water, wind, inland flood cues, nearby road trouble) and adjusts a bit if an official evacuation zone matches your pin. A low / green value means those feeds don’t show elevated concern in this app’s model right now — not that a storm can’t happen. See How the score works for a short breakdown.

Dashboard vs Home risk

Dashboard uses a map pin (or default coordinates) for a quick regional snapshot. Evacuation & traffic is a companion page focused on zones, nearby road feeds, and a checklist. Home risk (after sign-in) geocodes a specific address, saves profiles, and adds context like evacuation zone lookup and traffic near that pin.

Why do I need an account for Home risk?

So you can save addresses and snapshots without putting that data in a shared browser-only cache. Credentials stay on the server you run (local SQLite by default).

What emails can I get?

Signed in? Open Alerts & news in the navigation. Save preferences with the Save email preferences button (verified email required for sends). You can get tier-increase notices, evacuation-related updates tied to saved homes, and a sample evacuation-style email. None of this replaces NWS or your county.

What is the robot button?

When you’re signed in, the guide answers questions using the data on your current page — dashboard, home assessment, or Evacuation & traffic — plus short general tips elsewhere. It can be wrong; treat it as a second opinion, not authority. “Ask AI” under topic dropdowns is a one-shot summary per topic per snapshot to save cost.

Where does the data come from?

Public weather, water, river, and road feeds (for example NWS, NOAA, USGS, Open-Meteo, FDOT-style layers, and county GIS for evacuation zones). Data can lag or disagree — always confirm with official sources.
